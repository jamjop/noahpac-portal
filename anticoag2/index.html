Anticoagulation Reversal

Anticoagulant & antiplatelet reversal, periprocedural bridging. ↻ Reviewed quarterly

Life-threatening bleeding: Do not wait for labs to start reversal in a hemodynamically unstable patient. Give reversal agent, activate massive transfusion protocol if indicated, and pursue source control (endoscopy, surgery, IR) in parallel. Hematology consult for complex or refractory cases.

Warfarin Reversal by Severity / INR

Life-threatening bleeding
Any INR. 4F-PCC (Kcentra) 25–50 units/kg based on INR and weight (per product labeling) plus Vitamin K 10 mg IV, given as a slow infusion (not push — risk of anaphylactoid reaction).
Major bleeding, not life-threatening
4F-PCC (preferred, faster correction) or FFP if PCC unavailable, plus Vitamin K 5–10 mg IV or PO.
INR > 10, no significant bleeding
Hold warfarin. Oral Vitamin K 2.5–5 mg. Recheck INR in 24h.
INR 4.5–10, no significant bleeding
Hold warfarin. Low-dose oral Vitamin K (1–2.5 mg) if high bleeding risk factors present; otherwise hold and recheck INR — routine Vitamin K not required for every patient in this range.
INR < 4.5, no significant bleeding
Hold or reduce dose. Vitamin K not routinely needed.

Periprocedural Bridging

Low–moderate thromboembolic risk
Most non-valvular AFib (CHA₂DS₂-VASc without prior stroke/TIA), provoked VTE >3 months prior. No bridging — hold anticoagulant per timing below, resume post-procedure once hemostasis is adequate. The BRIDGE trial showed bridging increases bleeding without reducing thromboembolic events in this group.
High thromboembolic risk
Mechanical mitral valve, any mechanical valve + prior stroke/TIA, VTE within 3 months, CHA₂DS₂-VASc with recent stroke. Bridge with therapeutic-dose LMWH or UFH while warfarin is held. DOACs generally still do not require bridging due to short half-life.
Warfarin hold timing
Stop 5 days prior to procedure. Check INR the day before (or day of) — give small dose of oral Vitamin K if still elevated. Resume within 24h post-procedure if hemostasis is adequate.
DOAC hold timing (apixaban/rivaroxaban)
Low bleed-risk procedure: hold 1–2 days. High bleed-risk procedure: hold 2–3 days. Extend by 1–2 days if CrCl <50 mL/min (longer for dabigatran, which is more renally dependent). Resume 24h (low-risk) to 48–72h (high-risk) post-procedure.

General Principles

Time since last dose
DOAC half-lives are short (~12h for most). Reversal may be unnecessary for minor bleeding if >2–3 half-lives have elapsed since the last dose. For major or life-threatening bleeding, reverse regardless of timing.
Renal function
All DOACs are partly renally cleared (dabigatran most, ~80%). Impaired renal function prolongs drug exposure and increases both bleeding risk and the likely need for reversal/dialysis consideration.
Dialyzability
Dabigatran is dialyzable (low protein binding). Apixaban, rivaroxaban, and edoxaban are highly protein-bound and not effectively removed by dialysis — reversal agent or PCC is the primary strategy.
When in doubt
Involve hematology early for refractory bleeding, combined anticoagulant/antiplatelet therapy, hepatic impairment, or when the reversal response is inadequate. Local hospital massive transfusion and reversal protocols take precedence over this general reference.
